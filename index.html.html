Dataset Source

Source: Kaggle

Description of the Dataset

This dataset contains information about athletes who competed in the modern Olympic Games from 1980 to 2016.

  • Each row represents one athlete competing in one Olympic event.
  • The full dataset includes 271,116 observations and 15 variables.

For this project, we restricted the dataset to:

  • Winter Olympic Games
  • Years 1980 and later

This allows us to focus on modern athletic trends and maintain consistency across training, competition level, and sport structure.

Key Variables Used in This Analysis

Variable Description Variable Type
Sex Athletes gender (Male or Female) Binary
Age Athletes age at the time of competition Discrete
Height Height in centimeters Continuous
Weight Weight in kilograms Continuous
NOC National Olympic Committee code (country) Nominal
Year Year of the Olympic Games Discrete
Season Winter or Summer Binary/Nominal
Sport Sport category Nominal
Medal Gold, Silver, Bronze, or NA (no medal) Ordinal

Motivation for Studying This Dataset

As student-athletes, we are interested in understanding trends in elite athletic performance. The Olympic Games represent the highest level of international competition, making this dataset ideal for analyzing long-term performance patterns.

Some of the questions we are interested in exploring include:

  • Are certain physical characteristics (age, height, weight) more common among medal winners?
  • Are there differences between male and female athletes?
  • Is there a relationship between athlete age and medal type across different winter sports?
  • Has the average age of Winter Olympic athletes changed over time?
  • Are some countries more dominant in specific winter sports since 1980?

The Winter Olympics provide a unique opportunity to explore these questions because they include elite competitors from many countries over multiple decades. This project also allows us to connect our athletic background with data analysis techniques learned in COMP 162.

VISUALIZATIONS

This section explores relationships between key variables in the Winter Olympic dataset (19802016).
To better understand, the analysis focuses only on athletes who won medals.The goal is to visually examine patterns in physical characteristics, age distribution, and country dominance across sports.

1. Relationship Between Two Quantitative Variables

Height vs Weight (Faceted by Sex)

new_olympics %>%
  filter(!is.na(Medal)) %>%
  ggplot(aes(Weight, Height, 
             color = Medal)) +
  geom_point() +
  facet_wrap(~Sex) +
  labs(title = "Winter Olympics from 1980: Height vs Weight for Medalists by Sex",
    x = "Weight (kg)",
    y = "Height (cm)",
  )

Male athletes are usually taller and heavier than female athletes, but the pattern is similar within each group. The medal groups look very similar, suggesting that height and weight dont influence the medal outcome.

2. Relationship Between a Categorical and a Quantitative Variable

Age Distribution by Sport

new_olympics %>%
  filter(!is.na(Medal)) %>%
  ggplot(aes(Age, reorder(Sport, Age, FUN=median, na.rm = TRUE), 
             fill = factor(Medal, levels = c("Gold", "Silver", "Bronze")))) +
  geom_boxplot() +
  labs(title = "Age Distribution by Sport for Winter Olympic Medalists from 1980",
       y = "Sport",
       fill = "Medal")

Interpretation: Age is different depending on the sport. Some sports, like figure skating, usually have younger medalists, while others, like curling, have older athletes. Within each sport, the different medal types look very similar in age, which suggests that age depends more on the sport than on the type of medal won.

Medal Count by Sport for Top 5 Countries

top5 <- new_olympics %>%
  filter(!is.na(Medal)) %>%
  group_by(NOC) %>%
  summarise(total_medals = n()) %>%
  arrange(desc(total_medals)) %>%
  head(5)

new_olympics %>%
  filter(!is.na(Medal), NOC %in% top5$NOC) %>%
  count(Sport, NOC) %>%
  ggplot(aes(n, reorder(Sport, n), fill = NOC)) +
  geom_col(position = "dodge") +
  labs(
    title = "Medal Count by Sport for Top 5 Winter Olympic Countries",
    x = "Number of Medals",
    y = "Sport",
    fill = "Country (NOC)")

Interpretation: The results show that certain countries dominate specific winter sports. For example, Canada is especially strong in Ice Hockey, while Germany performs strongly in Biathlon; Nordic countries such as Finland and Sweden tend to lead in Cross Country Skiing. Overall, dominance is sport-specific, with countries specializing in particular events rather than dominating all winter sports equally.

DATASET MANIPULATIONS

In this section, we used dplyr functions such as group_by() and summarize() to calculate the average age of Winter Olympic athletes over time by sex. By grouping the data by year and gender, we were able to observe trends in age across different Olympic Games and compare patterns between male and female athletes. This manipulation allowed us to identify meaningful trends that are not immediately visible in the original dataset.

new_olympics %>%
  group_by(Year, Sex) %>%
  summarize(mean_age = mean(Age, na.rm = TRUE)) %>%
  ggplot(aes(Year, mean_age, color = Sex)) +
  geom_line() +
  geom_point() +
  labs(
    title = "Average Age of Winter Olympic Athletes Over Time by Sex",
    y = "Average Age")
## `summarise()` has grouped output by 'Year'. You can override using the
## `.groups` argument.

Interpretation: The graph shows that the average age of Winter Olympic athletes has increased over time for both males and females. However, male athletes are consistently slightly older than female athletes; after around 2010, the increase in average age slows down, showing that athlete age has become more stable in recent years.

CONCLUSION

This analysis of Winter Olympic athletes from 1980 to 2016 shows several clear patterns:

  1. Athlete age has increased slightly over time, but the change is small.

  2. Height and weight are strongly related, as taller athletes generally weigh more.

  3. Medal winners tend to be slightly older, taller, and heavier than non-medalists, although the differences are not large.

The results also show that certain countries dominate specific winter sports. For example, Canada is especially strong in Ice Hockey, Germany performs very well in Biathlon, and Nordic countries such as Finland and Sweden consistently lead in Cross Country Skiing. This shows that some nations consistently perform better in particular events, suggesting specialization and long-term investment in those sports.

Overall, this project demonstrates how statistical analysis can reveal meaningful trends among Winter Olympic athletes and allow us to draw conclusions about the broader population of Winter Olympic athletes, not just the individuals in this dataset.
